## The mental model shift

You have spent years mastering TCP/IP, BGP, spanning tree, and QoS.
You can troubleshoot a flapping OSPF adjacency without looking at documentation.
But when you walk into an AI data center for the first time, the familiar mental
models stop working.

In enterprise networking, a 1ms latency spike is a footnote in a monitoring
dashboard. In an AI training cluster running a large language model across 256
GPUs, that same 1ms spike causes every GPU in the job to sit idle — waiting for
one straggler. That idle time compounds across thousands of training steps per
hour. The network is not just moving data. It is the synchronisation mechanism
for a massively parallel computation.

Here is how the requirements compare:

| Dimension | Enterprise networking | HPC / AI networking |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Latency target | 1–10ms acceptable | Sub-2 microseconds required |
| Packet loss | Rare, recoverable with TCP | Zero tolerance — stalls entire job |
| Bandwidth | Shared, oversubscribed | Dedicated, non-blocking |
| Congestion | QoS queuing and dropping | Prevented with lossless fabric |
| Switch role | Pure forwarding plane | Active compute participant |
| NIC role | Commodity, kernel-attached | Active co-processor, kernel-bypassed |

## The AllReduce problem

AI model training splits work across many GPUs simultaneously. After each
training step, all GPUs must agree on updated parameters. This requires an
operation called AllReduce: every GPU sends its gradient updates to all other
GPUs, they are summed, and the result goes back to all GPUs before the next
step can begin.

That synchronisation point — the AllReduce — is a barrier. No GPU can proceed
to the next step until every other GPU has finished. If one GPU's network path
has a retransmission due to a dropped packet, every other GPU sits idle waiting.

At 1000 training steps per hour on a 256-GPU cluster, one bad switch port
can waste hundreds of GPU-hours per day at significant cost.

## What makes this hard to learn

HPC networking knowledge lives in:

- Vendor runbooks that are not publicly available
- Pre-sales conversations with NVIDIA field engineers
- Conference papers written for researchers, not practitioners
- Internal documentation at hyperscalers

There is no Packet Tracer equivalent for InfiniBand or RoCEv2.
This platform is built to fill that gap.
